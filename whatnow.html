The discovery

My name is Martha Willow and I am an author, professor and historian specialized in the history of Nova Scotia in the 18th century.

A few weeks ago I found an old envelope tightly folded in the spine of an old Bible under the eaves of a barn that was being demolished near a village called Lunenburg, on the South Shore of Nova Scotia.

The envelope contained a letter and two maps, hidden there by a local man called William Loffer who was taken captive alongside John Creighton during the Sack of Lunenburg in July 1782. They travelled together to Boston where they were taken to wait for the payment of a ransom. No ransom was paid and eventually they were sent back to Lunenburg ... but at some point during the voyage, either in Boston or on the way back, they met with a mysterious man who called himself a 'raider' (adventurer?) who gave them what William Loffer describes as a 'mysterious and powerful artefact'.

I will let you read his letter, it will give you more information.



The search begins

I can't resist an old map and the letter was so intriguing that I immediately started working on the clues. Soon it was taking all my time so I put my own research on hold and call this new project Lunenburg Lost.

It took me a couple of months and a lot of reading and researching, even meeting my fair share of crusty, dusty archive keepers but I managed to work out the location of the four points William Loffer mentions in his letter. Shortly after, I was able to find the 'hidden instructions' he says he included ... and I also worked out the code he used to decrypt them.

Just as I was getting ready to figure out the location of the artefact and organise a trip to retrieve it, I received an offer from a very large multinational company to buy the letter and maps. I thought it was suspicious so I turned it down, at least until I found the artefact and understood what it was about. They increased their already considerable offer, and that made me downright worried and determined to keep everything to myself. Then a colleague whose research they were funding started threatening me and I thought I might be followed ... just shadows in the dark and footsteps behind me, so I can't be sure but when my office was broken into and then set on fire and I lost everything that was kept in it. Luckily, I had taken the Lunenburg Lost files home with me on the day of the fire, so they didn't burn, but it won't be long before they try again.

I asked a friend to recommend someone based in Lunenburg who I could trust and he gave me your name. And this is where you come in ...
